Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Yidong Shi, Haoshuo Bi, Yuntao San — yis089@ucsd.edu, hbi@ucsd.edu, joshan@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Countries with higher education levels tend to have older populations.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Normalized both series to 0–1: This makes the upward movement of education and aging directly comparable and clear to the reader.
  • Used a long time span (1970–2020): A broad view highlights a persistent relationship between the two indicators over time.
  • Applied one shared y-axis and subtle grid lines: Keeps focus on the two rising curves rather than visual clutter.
  • Neutral title and concise annotations: Tells the story without distracting or confusing the viewer.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Focused on a short window (1995–2000): The short time frame hides the long-term pattern and makes the data look random.
  • Keep axes in a tight range: Compressing the scale makes differences look smaller.
  • No trend line or extra labels: Leaves viewers without clues about direction.
  • Neutral title (“the link looks weak”): Sounds objective but frames the data as unrelated.

Final Reflection

Designing for both sides showed how framing alone—normalization, time-window selection, and subsetting—can pivot a reader’s takeaway without altering the underlying data. In the long-term line chart, using a full 50-year scale and normalized axes visually reinforces co-movement, making the relationship appear structural. In contrast, the short-window scatter plot limits the scale and sample, flattening the trend and suggesting that correlation may depend on framing.

We define ethical visualization as disclosing transformations that materially affect interpretation and avoiding moves that a reasonable reader cannot easily detect. Acceptable persuasion clarifies an argument while surfacing caveats (e.g., long-horizon trends with annotations). Misleading choices conceal or exaggerate those caveats, manufacturing impressions that would not hold under transparent framing. Our “against” design deliberately employs a mild form of deception (narrow window + subset) to reveal the fragility of visual conclusions and to model responsible disclosure in captions and write-ups.
